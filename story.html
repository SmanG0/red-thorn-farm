The story

A small kitchen.
A slow fire.
One good idea.

Food, Philipp will tell you, is a story before it is a meal and Red Thorn Farm is the long way of telling one.

It begins, like most good kitchens, with a fire that someone forgot to put out. A Dutch farmyard in winter, a chef called Martijn, a slab of pork hanging from an iron hook, and a young Tyrolean cook called Philipp Achammer being handed a bundle of fresh thyme and told, simply, to wait.

"Wait" is not a word young cooks like very much. Modern kitchens run on instant instant smoke, instant brown, instant plate. But the people at De Vuurplaats, the open-fire kitchen on Landgoed Vollenhoven where Philipp trained, believe in something older than that. They believe in coals you can see your own face in. In meat that is touched ten times before it is served. In flavour that comes from being patient.

Years later, on a piece of red-soil farmland off the Magadi Road in Kenya, that lesson became Red Thorn.

Trained by fire

Before Kenya there was a farm in De Bilt, the Netherlands a wooden barn, a long table under the trees, and a chef who insisted that the kitchen belonged outside. Philipp spent his evenings there learning how to cook over wood, how to listen to a piece of pork crackle, how to read smoke.

The work was simple and slow. Whole animals broken down by hand. Salt rubs left for days at a time. Hours spent feeding a fire instead of a deadline. He learned to slap green herbs onto meat in fat handfuls, to throw a few branches of bay on the coals just to change the air, to walk away from a roast and trust it.

When he moved back to Kenya where his father had farmed since the eighties he brought one thing with him: the idea that good food is mostly a question of good time. Everything else, he reasoned, you could probably build.

"There are five ingredients in a Red Thorn sausage. None of them are surprising. The work is in not adding the sixth."

Why Kenya, why now

There is a particular kind of meat you can buy in Nairobi if you know where to look: beef from grass-fed Boran cattle in Laikipia, pork from small holdings in the Rift Valley, lamb from the Kedong plains where the wind comes off the Suswa craters and the grazing is still wild. It is some of the best in Africa. It also rarely makes it past the slaughterhouse without being turned into something less.

The supermarket sausage pink, smooth, full of rusk and water is everywhere. The honest one, made the long way, with whole cuts and whole spices and time, is almost nowhere. That gap is the size of a small business, and Red Thorn is what filled it.

We started with a hand-built smoker on a corner of the farm, a butcher's block bought second-hand in Industrial Area, and a list of four sausages we knew we could make properly. The first batch went to friends. The second batch went to friends of friends. By the fourth we had a waiting list, which felt, in the polite Kenyan sense, like a sign.

How it built

  1. 2018

    An apprenticeship in Tyrol

    Philipp begins butchery training in the South Tyrolean Alps the kind of villages where every household still has a smokehouse and a cellar full of speck.

  2. 2021

    De Vuurplaats, the Netherlands

    A summer in De Bilt becomes a residency. Asado-style fire cooking, whole-animal butchery, and an obsession with the slow lane that he doesn't quite shake.

  3. 2023

    Back to the farm, off Magadi Road

    Philipp returns to Kenya, builds a smoker out of scrap steel and acacia, and starts cooking for whoever happens to be on the verandah that weekend.

  4. 2024

    Red Thorn Farm opens for orders

    First batch leaves the farm in October. Four sausages, twenty-six links, two cooler boxes, and a WhatsApp list that grew faster than expected.

  5. 2026

    Where we are now

    Weekly batches, five varieties, delivered Nairobi-wide in 2-5 business days.

What we believe

A short list, and we mean every one of them.

  1. i.

    Whole cuts, never trim.

    Every sausage is built from cuts we could roast as they are shoulder, belly, leg. Nothing reclaimed, nothing rebuilt out of by-product.

  2. ii.

    Five-ingredient rule.

    Meat, fat, salt, spice, casing. If the recipe doesn't fit, the recipe is wrong. No nitrates, no fillers, no preservatives, no rusk.

  3. iii.

    Real smoke, real wood.

    Acacia, cut on the farm, dried for months. No liquid smoke, no smoke flavouring, no shortcut bottles in the cupboard.

  4. iv.

    Hands, not machines.

    Cased by hand, tied by hand, weighed by hand. The links are a little uneven on purpose that's how you know nobody automated it.

  5. v.

    Small enough to know your name.

    We make what we can make properly in a week and no more. The waiting list is a feature, not a bug.

The people

There is not a large team behind Red Thorn. There is Philipp, who does the butchery, the curing, the smoking and most of the talking. There is Esther, who runs the kitchen side packaging, deliveries, the order book, and the answering of the WhatsApp. There is Joseph, who keeps the smoker fed and the yard in order. And there are the farmers Ole Nkurrunah in Kedong, Mwangi up in Laikipia, the Patels in Naivasha whose names go on the back of every package, because we think they should.

The long way around

There is no clever ending to a story like this. The cooking is the same on Tuesday as it was a thousand Tuesdays ago in a Tyrolean cellar salt, time, smoke, patience, and a quiet sort of belief that nothing here needs to be hurried.

If you've read this far, that probably suits you too. Pull up a chair. The fire is on.

Philipp Achammer
Founder · butcher · keeper of the fire
